Agro-Industry Financing in Côte d’Ivoire
State of Play 2026

Jean Élie N’Goran, Managing Partner, Africand Consulting May 2026
←  All Insights
Also available in  Français Lire en Français →

Executive Summary

Côte d’Ivoire remains the most active market for agro-industrial financing in West Africa. This note maps the active lenders, their sector focus, typical ticket sizes, and the structural gaps that persist despite growing institutional appetite. Designed for operators, investors and practitioners preparing mandates in 2026.

I

The Market in Numbers

Côte d’Ivoire’s agro-industrial sector accounts for approximately 40% of national export revenues. The cocoa value chain alone generates an estimated $4.2bn annually at farm gate, with processing contributing a growing share as the government’s 50% processing target policy drives investment upstream.

Financed transactions in the sector grew by an estimated 22% in volume between 2023 and 2025, driven by three factors: increasing DFI allocations to food systems, private equity appetite for midstream processing assets, and a small but growing cohort of bankable domestic operators.

II

III

Where the Gaps Persist

Three financing gaps remain structurally underserved in the current landscape. First, the $3M to $8M range below most DFI direct deal thresholds and above most microfinance capacity. This is where the majority of growth-stage agro-industrial SMEs sit, and where the supply of appropriate capital is thinnest.

Second, working capital and trade finance for seasonal agricultural cycles. The structural mismatch between a cocoa or cashew operator’s cash cycle and the repayment schedules of most formal finance instruments creates chronic liquidity constraints that inhibit scaling.

Third, greenfield processing infrastructure below $15M. DFIs will finance expansion but are structurally cautious on first-of-kind assets in new locations. This is where blended finance structures combining concessional and commercial capital offer the most credible solution, and where specialist advisory is most critical.

IV

What This Means for Operators in 2026

The financing environment in Côte d’Ivoire is more favourable than at any point in the past decade. Institutional appetite is real. But appetite without preparation does not produce transactions.

Operators planning to raise in 2026 should begin documentation preparation now. Audited financials, a credible financial model, and a clear articulation of use of proceeds these are table stakes. The operators who will close in 2026 are the ones who started preparing in 2025.
