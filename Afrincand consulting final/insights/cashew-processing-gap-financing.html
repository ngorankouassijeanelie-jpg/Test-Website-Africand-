The Cashew Processing Gap
A Financing Perspective

Jean Élie N'Goran, Managing Partner, Africand Consulting April 2026
←  All Insights

Executive Summary

Côte d’Ivoire produces 45% of global raw cashew supply yet processes less than 12% domestically. The gap is not a production problem. It is a financing problem. This note examines the specific barriers to cashew processing investment, identifies where capital is available, and proposes the financing structures most likely to close transactions in the current environment.

I

The Scale of the Gap

Côte d’Ivoire exports approximately 800,000 tonnes of raw cashew annually. At current world market prices, domestic processing of 50% of that volume would generate an additional $600M to $900M in export revenues per year a transformation in value retention that would rank among the most significant economic interventions available to the country.

The government’s 2030 processing target of 50% is well known. The financing pathway to reach it is not. The following numbers frame the challenge.

45% of global raw cashew production from Côte d’Ivoire
12% processed domestically today
$1.8B estimated capex required to reach 50% processing target
II

Why Financing Has Not Followed Ambition

The investment case for cashew processing is clear. The raw material is available and price-competitive. There is established international demand for processed product. The government offers fiscal incentives for processing investment. And yet the pace of financed transactions remains far below what the opportunity warrants.

Africand’s analysis identifies five specific barriers:

  • — Operator fragmentation. The majority of cashew processors in Côte d’Ivoire operate at sub-scale, with annual processing volumes below the threshold at which most DFIs will engage directly. Consolidation or aggregation is a prerequisite for institutional financing.
  • — Off-take uncertainty. International buyers require quality certification, consistent supply and documented traceability. Most domestic processors cannot yet provide all three. This is not a permanent condition, it is a preparation problem that specialist advisory can solve.
  • — Equipment financing gaps. Processing equipment for cashew largely imported from India, Vietnam and Brazil requires hard currency financing that is structurally difficult for domestic operators to access at competitive rates.
  • — Working capital cycles. The cashew harvest is seasonal. Processors need working capital to purchase raw nuts at harvest and hold inventory through the processing season. Most bank financing in the region is too short-term and too expensive for this cycle.
  • — Bankability of first transactions. For new entrants to the sector, the absence of a trading history makes it structurally difficult to access formal finance. The first financing is always the hardest. After two or three closed seasons, the path widens considerably.

“The investment case is clear. The financing pathway is not. That is precisely the gap Africand was built to close.”

III

Where Capital Is Available Today

Despite the barriers, capital is available for cashew processing for operators who approach the right institutions with the right preparation. Three financing windows are currently active:

DFI equity and quasi-equity is available for operators with processing capacity above 5,000 tonnes per season, an existing off-take relationship with an international buyer, and a management team with demonstrable sector experience. Proparco, DEG and FMO are the most active institutions in this window.

Blended finance structures combining concessional first-loss capital from impact funds with commercial debt are available for smaller operators (1,000 to 5,000 tonnes) who can demonstrate smallholder supply chain linkages. Norfund, BIO and certain ECOWAS-backed funds are active in this space.

Trade finance facilities structured against confirmed off-take contracts are available from a limited set of regional commercial banks and international trade finance providers, typically at tenors of 90 to 180 days. This is the most accessible window for first-time operators and requires the least preparation time.

IV

The Africand Perspective

We have structured and closed cashew processing transactions across the size spectrum described above. The path from “viable business” to “financed transaction” is navigable. It requires the right preparation, the right counterpart identification, and an advisor who knows the sector at the level of the individual transaction not at the level of a country strategy document.
