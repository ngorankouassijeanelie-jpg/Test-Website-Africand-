Why West African Agro-Industry
Still Can’t Access DFI Capital

Jean Élie N’Goran, Managing Partner, Africand Consulting June 2026
←  All Insights

Executive Summary

Despite decades of development finance institution activity in West Africa, the majority of agro-industrial operators in the region remain unable to access DFI capital. The barrier is not appetite. It is preparation. This note identifies the four structural reasons behind the gap and what practitioners must do differently.

I

The Appetite Is Real. The Gap Is Real.

Development finance institutions the IFC, AfDB, Proparco, FMO, DEG and their bilateral counterparts have collectively committed to increasing agricultural value chain investment in West Africa. The mandates are there. The capital is allocated. The pipelines are described in annual reports and investor roadshows.

And yet, the vast majority of agro-industrial operators in Côte d’Ivoire, Ghana, Senegal and Burkina Faso cannot access it. Not because they lack ambition. Not because their businesses are unviable. But because the distance between “investable in principle” and “bankable in practice” is larger than any single operator can cross alone.

“The distance between investable in principle and bankable in practice is larger than any single operator can cross alone.”

II

Four Structural Barriers

Documentation standards. DFIs require financial models, environmental and social assessments, legal due diligence packages and audited accounts that conform to international standards. Most West African agro-industrial SMEs produce none of these not because they are poorly run, but because the local advisory infrastructure to produce them at the required standard simply does not exist at accessible cost.

Ticket size mismatch. The majority of meaningful agro-industrial investments in the region fall between $5m and $30m. Most DFIs operate below $10m only through fund structures or co-investment vehicles, and their internal processing costs make sub-$15m direct deals economically inefficient. The result is a structural gap at the scale where the most transactions live.

Relationship asymmetry. DFI investment officers rotate on two-to-three year cycles. The institutional knowledge of specific markets, specific crops, specific operators does not accumulate at the lender level. It accumulates with local advisors who are present continuously, and who are rarely in the room when mandates are designed.

Risk perception lag. DFI credit committees rely on country risk ratings, sector precedent transactions and peer benchmarks. In markets where the precedent transaction set is thin, the absence of comparable deals becomes its own barrier. The first transaction in a new structure is always the hardest to close.

III

What Changes the Equation

The operators who successfully close DFI transactions share three characteristics. They have prepared their documentation to institutional standards before approaching any lender. They have engaged an advisor with existing DFI relationships and a demonstrated understanding of what each institution will and will not finance. And they have structured their ask to fit the investment mandate of the counterpart not the other way around.

This is not complex. But it requires preparation time, advisory support, and a realistic understanding of the process. The transactions that fail are almost always those where an operator has arrived at a DFI with an unstructured ask and no intermediary to translate between the business reality and the investment framework.

IV

The Africand Position

We structure agro-industrial mandates to the standard that DFIs expect. We know which institutions are active in which sectors and at which ticket sizes. We have closed transactions across the documentation-to-close lifecycle in Côte d’Ivoire, Senegal, Ghana and Burkina Faso.

The gap between West African agro-industry and development finance capital is not permanent. It is a preparation and intermediation problem. And preparation and intermediation problems have solutions.
